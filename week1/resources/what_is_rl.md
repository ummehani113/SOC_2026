# What is Reinforcement Learning?

A gentle introduction. No math, no code, just ideas.

## The Big Idea

Reinforcement Learning (RL) is how a computer learns to **make good decisions by trying things and seeing what happens**.

That's it. That's the whole field in one sentence.

Everything else — PPO, MADDPG, value functions, advantages — is just clever machinery for doing that one thing well.

## A Story You Already Know

Imagine teaching a dog to sit.

- You say "sit."
- The dog does *something* — maybe sits, maybe spins, maybe ignores you.
- If it sits, you give a treat. If not, no treat.
- Over time, the dog learns: "the word 'sit' + sitting = treat."

You never told the dog *how* to sit. You never explained the muscle movements. You just gave it feedback ("treat" or "no treat") and let it figure things out.

**That is reinforcement learning.** Replace the dog with a computer program, and the treats with numbers (rewards), and you've got the entire field.

## The Five Words You Need to Know

These show up everywhere in RL. Learn them now and the rest becomes much easier.

### 1. Agent
The thing that's learning. The decision-maker. In our story, the dog. In a video game, the player. In a self-driving car, the driving software.

### 2. Environment
Everything the agent interacts with but doesn't control. The world. In our story, you and the room. For a chess AI, the chessboard and the opponent.

### 3. State
A snapshot of "what's going on right now." For a chess AI: the current board position. For a self-driving car: positions of nearby cars, the speed, what the cameras see.

The agent sees the state and uses it to decide what to do.

### 4. Action
What the agent chooses to do. For the dog: sit, spin, lie down. For a chess AI: which piece to move where. For a trading bot: buy, sell, or hold.

### 5. Reward
A number the environment gives back telling the agent "that was good" or "that was bad." Higher is better. The dog's treat = +1. Knocking over a vase = -10.

**The agent's only job: choose actions to maximize total reward over time.**

## The Loop

Here's what happens, over and over:

```
   ┌──────────────────────────────┐
   │                              ↓
[Agent] ──action──→ [Environment]
   ↑                              │
   └────state, reward────────────┘
```

1. Agent looks at the **state**
2. Agent picks an **action**
3. Environment changes, gives back a new **state** and a **reward**
4. Repeat. Forever (or until the game ends).

A single run through this loop until something ends (game over, time's up, goal reached) is called an **episode**.

## How is This Different From Other Machine Learning?

You've probably heard of supervised learning — the kind where you show a model 10,000 cat photos labeled "cat" and 10,000 dog photos labeled "dog," and it learns to tell them apart.

**Supervised learning has labels.** Someone tells the model the right answer.

**RL has no labels.** Nobody tells the agent "this action was correct." The agent only sees the reward, which is often delayed and sparse.

Imagine learning chess where the only feedback is "you won" or "you lost" at the end of a 60-move game. Which move actually caused the win? That's the **credit assignment problem**, and it's at the heart of why RL is hard.

| | Supervised Learning | Reinforcement Learning |
|---|---|---|
| Feedback | Correct answer for every input | A reward number, often delayed |
| Goal | Predict the label | Maximize total reward |
| Data | Fixed dataset given to you | Generated by interacting |
| Example | Spam filter | Game-playing AI |

## What is a "Policy"?

A **policy** is the agent's strategy. It's a rule that says "in this state, do this action."

Some policies are simple:
- "Always go right." (bad chess strategy)
- "If the pole is leaning left, push left." (decent CartPole strategy)

Some policies are complex — like a neural network with millions of parameters that takes in a board position and outputs a chess move.

**The whole point of RL is to find a good policy.** Algorithms like PPO and SAC are just different recipes for searching for good policies.

## Exploration vs. Exploitation

Here's a tension at the heart of RL:

- **Exploit:** "I know action A gives me +5. I'll keep doing A."
- **Explore:** "Maybe action B gives me +10, but I've never tried it. Let me try."

If you only exploit, you might get stuck doing something mediocre. If you only explore, you waste time on bad things instead of using what you know.

Good RL agents balance both. This shows up everywhere — in recommender systems ("show this user the same kind of video, or try something new?"), in trading, in robotics.

## What Kinds of Problems Is RL Good For?

RL shines when:

1. **Decisions matter over time** — chess, robot walking, driving
2. **You can simulate or interact cheaply** — games, simulators
3. **You can define a clear reward** — score, profit, time-to-goal
4. **You don't know the "right answer"** in advance — there's no expert demonstration

RL is **not** great when:
- Data is fixed and labels are available → use supervised learning
- Rewards are impossible to define → use imitation learning
- You can't afford to try bad actions (medicine, real trading) → be very careful

## A Few Famous RL Successes

- **AlphaGo (2016)** beat the world champion at Go. Used RL + self-play.
- **OpenAI Five (2018)** played Dota 2 at pro level.
- **Robot hands** that learn to manipulate objects (OpenAI, 2019).
- **ChatGPT** uses RL from human feedback (RLHF) to be helpful.
- **Data center cooling** at Google — RL controls cooling systems, saves energy.

## Why Are We Using It for Trading?

Financial markets are an almost perfect RL playground:

- ✅ Clear state (prices, order book)
- ✅ Clear actions (buy, sell, hold, place orders)
- ✅ Clear reward (profit/loss)
- ✅ You can simulate and replay
- ⚠️ But: real markets have other learning agents, regimes change, and stakes are real

That last point — that there are **other agents** also learning — is what makes multi-agent RL hard. And it's what Weeks 2–4 are about.

For Week 1, just hold this in your head: **markets are environments, traders are agents, profits are rewards.** The rest is detail.

## A Mental Model to Carry Forward

When you read about any RL algorithm later, ask:

1. What is the **agent** here?
2. What's the **environment**?
3. What's a **state**, an **action**, a **reward**?
4. What's the **policy**?
5. How does this algorithm make the policy better over time?

If you can answer those five, you understand the algorithm — even if the math feels intimidating.
